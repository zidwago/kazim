## Preliminary Orientation

Most philosophical engagement with artificial intelligence begins with ethics: alignment, bias, autonomy, rights. These are important questions, but they are not the only philosophical questions, and they may not be the most fundamental ones.

What interests me is something prior: *what kind of thing is an LLM doing when it produces language?* This is not a question about consciousness or sentience — it is a question about the relationship between language production and reasoning, between statistical pattern-matching and what Wilfrid Sellars called "the space of reasons."

## The Sambrotta Connection

Mirco Sambrotta's paper "LLMs and the Logical Space of Reasons" is the first thing I've encountered that brings the Pittsburgh school — Sellars, Brandom, McDowell — into direct conversation with the AI literature. I haven't yet worked through it carefully, but the basic move is promising: rather than asking whether LLMs *think*, ask whether what they do can be located within the normative structure that Brandom calls the "game of giving and asking for reasons."

## Open Questions

These are genuinely open for me. I don't have positions on them yet.

- Is there a meaningful distinction between *using* language and *understanding* it, or does sufficient use constitute understanding? (This is roughly the Chinese Room problem, but I want to approach it through Brandom rather than Searle.)
- What does Leibniz's concept of *expression* — the structural mirroring between different levels of reality — look like when applied to neural networks?
- Does the philosophy of technology literature (which I'm only beginning to survey) offer resources that the philosophy of mind literature doesn't?
