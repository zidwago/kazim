## Why This Paper

This paper appears to bring the Pittsburgh school — Sellars, Brandom, McDowell — into conversation with large language models. That intersection is exactly what I'm looking for in my philosophy of AI research: not the usual consciousness/sentience framing, but the question of whether LLMs can be understood as participants (or pseudo-participants) in what Brandom calls the "game of giving and asking for reasons."

## Key Questions I'm Bringing to It

- Does Sambrotta think LLMs are *in* the space of reasons, or merely mimic being in it? What hangs on this distinction?
- How does this relate to Brandom's inferentialism — if meaning is constituted by inferential relations, and an LLM can track those relations statistically, does it thereby grasp meaning?
- What would Sellars say? The "Myth of the Given" seems relevant — is the training data a kind of "Given" for the model?
