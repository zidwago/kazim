Genealogy & Historicism

active 16/04/2025

Developing a view on the relationship between philosophy and its history — working through genealogical method, historicism, and the question of whether and how the origins of beliefs bear on their justification.

The Central Question

Does it matter, philosophically, where our beliefs and practices come from? Genealogical philosophy says yes — that tracing the historical origins of a belief can reveal something about its status. Anti-genealogical arguments (like Hanno Sauer’s) say no — that origins are epistemically irrelevant.

I think the answer is more complicated than either camp allows, but I don’t yet have a fully worked-out position. This project is where I’m developing one.

Key Texts

  • Friedrich Nietzsche, On the Genealogy of Morality — partially read, was blown away by it
  • Hanno Sauer, “The End of History” (Inquiry) — read, represents a very different view from my own approach
  • Amia Srinivasan, “Genealogy of Belief” syllabus — want to work through systematically
  • Robert Brandom, Tales of the Mighty Dead — interested in Brandom’s idea that engaging with the history of philosophy is itself a philosophical activity

Connections

This project connects to all my other research threads:

  • Leibniz and Hegel — my work in the history of philosophy is itself a kind of implicit commitment to historicism. Articulating that commitment explicitly is part of what this project is for.
  • Philosophy of AI — the question of how we understand novel intellectual phenomena (like LLMs) may itself have a genealogical dimension.
  • Spinoza — reading more Spinoza pairs naturally with the Leibniz work and broadens the rationalist context.
