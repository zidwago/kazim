Summary

Sauer argues against genealogical approaches in philosophy — the idea that tracing the historical origins of a belief or practice can tell us something about its justification. His view represents a challenge to the kind of historically-minded philosophy I’m drawn to.

What Struck Me

I was blown away by this paper even though (perhaps because) it represents a very different view from my own approach to philosophy. Sauer’s core claim — that genealogy is either epistemically irrelevant or collapses into standard epistemic evaluation — is sharp and well-argued.

The challenge for someone like me, who thinks the history of philosophy matters philosophically and not merely as antiquarian interest, is to articulate exactly how it matters in a way that survives Sauer’s critique.

Connections

  • This pairs naturally with Nietzsche’s Genealogy of Morality, which I’ve partially read and which represents the strongest case for genealogy as a philosophical method.
  • Amia Srinivasan’s “Genealogy of Belief” syllabus collects many of the key texts in this debate — I want to work through it systematically.
  • Brandom’s Tales of the Mighty Dead might offer resources here: his idea that engaging with the history of philosophy is itself a philosophical activity, not merely a historical one.
